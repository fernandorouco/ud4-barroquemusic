5. Women in the Baroque period

The great forgotten female composers!

5. WOMEN IN THE BAROQUE PERIOD

During the Baroque period, despite enormous social difficulties and explicit opposition from institutions such as the Church, several female composers managed to make their mark and leave a real legacy in the history of music. The emergence of periodicals that spread sheet music throughout Europe allowed their work to circulate beyond their immediate surroundings. These women composed at a time marked by the Inquisition, devastating epidemics and prejudices as strong as the papal ban that claimed music was harmful to female modesty. Even so, they created music of enormous quality and participated actively in the cultural life of their time.

5.1. Who were they and what did they compose?

In Italy, Francesca Caccini worked at the court of Florence with the highest salary of all the ducal musicians and became the first known woman to write opera. Her Liberazione di Ruggiero 1625) was even performed outside Italy. In 17th-century Venice, Barbara Strozzi published her works without the support of powerful patrons, and her scores travelled throughout Europe; Rosanna Scalfi, for her part, wrote cantatas that were performed on gondolas during summer nights.

In France, Elisabeth Jacquet de La Guerre managed to gain a foothold in the court of Louis XIV and premiered the first French opera composed by a woman. Julie Pinel published a collection of highly acclaimed arias in a widely circulated music magazine.

In the Germanic territories, Wilhelmine von Bayreuth composed opera, chamber music and obras instrumentales during a period in which she also promoted the construction of the famous Margrave Opera House.

In England, Elisabeth Turner published canciones in the prestigious magazine The Lady and worked as a singer in various cities.
